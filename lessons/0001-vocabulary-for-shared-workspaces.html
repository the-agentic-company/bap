Lesson 0001

Vocabulary for Shared Workspaces

A first pass at the nouns you need before designing groups of users inside your app.

Win for this lesson: you can look at a collaboration feature and decide whether it is an organization, workspace, team, group, department, role, or resource.

The mental model

Established collaboration tools tend to separate three ideas:

  1. Containers hold work and policy: organization, workspace, teamspace.
  2. Membership sets collect people: team, group, department.
  3. Permission scopes define what people can do: role, access level, visibility.

Linear says a workspace contains the company work model, and teams sit inside it. Notion uses teamspaces as dedicated areas for teams. Slack separates Enterprise organizations, workspaces, channels, and user groups. Google Workspace separates a hierarchical organizational unit tree from other group-like access concepts.

Organization

The company or customer boundary.

Billing, security policy, SSO, audit logs, and top-level admins usually live here.

Workspace

The shared product home.

This is where day-to-day work happens. In a small app, organization and workspace may appear as one user-facing thing.

Team

A visible group that works together.

Engineering, Design, Support, Growth. Teams are durable and understandable to members.

Group

A membership set for access or notifications.

Groups can be less social and more mechanical: approvers, beta testers, incident responders.

Department

A formal org-chart function.

Use this only when your app needs to mirror company structure or reporting lines.

Role

A capability at a scope.

Owner, admin, member, guest, viewer, editor. Roles say what a person can do.

A practical naming convention

For your app, start conservative:

Organization
  Workspace
    Team
    Group
    Resource
      Role assignment

If your first customers are small teams, you can hide Organization in the UI and show Workspace. If enterprise customers matter, keep both in the internal model: organization for policy and workspace for collaboration.

Practice

Choose the noun that best fits each product case. The point is not perfection; it is to learn what each noun is for.

Answer the cases, then check yourself.
Rule of thumb: do not model the company chart and the permission system as the same thing. Departments explain where people sit. Groups and roles explain what access they have.

Use this in your app

When naming a feature, ask two questions:

  1. Is this thing a place, a set of people, or a permission?
  2. Is this thing user-facing product language, or internal implementation language?

That prevents common naming mistakes such as calling an admin role a group, calling every team a workspace, or using tenant in the UI.

Review

Keep the Workspace Collaboration Glossary open while sketching your app model. Ask the agent follow-up questions when a distinction feels fuzzy; those fuzzy spots are usually where the product model needs sharper boundaries.
